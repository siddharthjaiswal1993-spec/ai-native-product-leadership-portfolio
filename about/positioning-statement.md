# Positioning Statement

**AI-native enterprise product leader who builds systems that detect, diagnose, decide, and verify — not just summarise.**

---

## What Makes an AI-Native PM Different

Most product managers approaching AI do one of two things: they wrap a chat interface around an LLM and call it a product, or they bolt AI onto an existing workflow and call it a feature. Neither creates durable value.

An AI-native PM starts from a different question: what decisions does a user need to make, and what would it take for software to reliably assist or execute those decisions at enterprise scale? That requires understanding not just what the model can do, but how to design the retrieval system, how to calibrate the confidence thresholds, how to define what success looks like without a ground truth label, how to build the approval layer that keeps humans accountable for high-stakes actions, and how to instrument the system so you can tell when it is drifting.

The skills that separate an AI-native PM from a traditional PM who has learned to use AI are: evaluation methodology, failure mode reasoning, trust architecture, human-in-the-loop design, and the ability to have a technical conversation with an ML engineer about why a retrieval pipeline is returning stale context. These are not soft skills. They are precision tools.

## What I Believe About Enterprise AI

Enterprise AI products that will win are not the ones with the most capable models. They are the ones with the deepest workflow integration, the most disciplined governance design, and the best-compounding organisational memory. Models are commoditising. Workflow design, trust architecture, and data flywheels are the moat.

I also believe that human-in-the-loop is not a liability — it is a product strategy. In regulated industries and high-stakes enterprise contexts, the products that earn trust are the ones that make human oversight easy, auditable, and meaningful. HITL done well is not friction; it is the feature that unlocks adoption.
